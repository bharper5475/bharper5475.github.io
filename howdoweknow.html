As he grew older, he began to take his musical studies more seriously, and he found that he had a natural talent for composition and arrangement. But it wasn't until Ben began studying music theory in earnest that he began to notice something strange. He found himself becoming increasingly aroused by certain concepts, particularly the major scale. He couldn't explain it, but there was something about the way the notes fit together in that particular pattern that set his pulse racing. At first, Ben tried to ignore these feelings, chalking them up to a strange quirk of his imagination. But as time went on, he realized that this was a real and persistent attraction. He felt ashamed and confused, unsure of how to make sense of these new and unsettling desires. Ben spent many long hours searching the internet, trying to find someone who could help him make sense of what he was feeling. He discovered that there was a whole community of people who were attracted to all sorts of strange and unconventional things, from inanimate objects to mathematical formulas. They called themselves "objectum-sexuals," and Ben couldn't help but wonder if he might be one of them. But the more he read about objectum-sexuality, the more he realized that it didn't quite fit what he was feeling. He wasn't sure if there was a name for what he was feeling, or if he was the only one who felt this way. Ben tried talking to a few of his friends about what he was experiencing, but they didn't seem to understand. They thought he was joking, or maybe even a little bit crazy. It was clear that he wasn't going to find the answers he was looking for from the people around him. So Ben turned back to the internet, and this time he stumbled upon a forum specifically for people who were attracted to music theory. It was like a whole new world had opened up to him. There were people there who felt the same way he did, who could understand what it was like to be turned on by the sound of a perfectly resolved cadence or the mathematical beauty of a harmonic progression. For the first time in a long time, Ben felt like he had found a place where he belonged. He spent hours reading through the forum threads, soaking up as much information as he could. He learned about other people's experiences and struggles, and he began to realize that he wasn't alone. But even with the support of the online community, Ben still felt a deep sense of shame and confusion about his sexuality. He wasn't sure how to come to terms with these new and unfamiliar desires, or how to integrate them into his life as a musician. He felt like he was standing on the edge of a precipice, unsure of whether to take the leap into the unknown or retreat back into the safety of his old, familiar world. For months, Ben struggled with the conflicting emotions that were swirling inside him. On the one hand, he was excited by the idea of exploring his attraction to music theory, of pushing the boundaries of what was considered "normal" and "acceptable." On the other hand, he was terrified of what people might think of him if they ever found out. He didn't want to be labeled as a freak or a pervert, or worse, to lose the respect of his friends and family. Despite these fears, however, Ben found himself unable to deny the power of his attraction. The Beatles had been one of his biggest musical influences, and he admired Lennon's creativity and courage as an artist and activist. One night, as Ben was working on a new composition, he felt a strange sensation in the room. It was like a sudden drop in temperature, and he could feel a faint presence that seemed to be watching him. He turned around, expecting to see someone standing behind him, but the room was empty. He tried to shake off the feeling, telling himself that he was just being paranoid. But as he continued to work on his music, he couldn't shake the feeling that he was being watched. It was like a chill that ran down his spine, and he knew that he couldn't ignore it any longer. He decided to take a break and went to make some tea in the kitchen. As he was waiting for the water to boil, he felt the same sensation again, like someone was standing right behind him. He turned around, but once again, there was no one there. That's when he heard it: a faint voice, almost like a whisper, coming from somewhere in the room. He strained his ears, trying to make out what it was saying, and then he heard it again: "Hey, man." It was a voice he knew well, even though he had never heard it in person before. It was the voice of John Lennon. Ben felt a rush of excitement and disbelief. He had never believed in ghosts or the paranormal, but here he was, apparently being visited by the spirit of one of his musical heroes. For a few moments, they just stood there, staring at each other in silence. Ben wasn't sure what to say or how to react, and he could feel his heart pounding in his chest. Finally, Lennon spoke again. "You're a talented kid," he said. "I like what you're doing with that composition. It's got a lot of heart." Ben was still in shock, but he managed to stammer out a response. "Thank you," he said. "I've always admired your music. It's an honor to meet you, even if you're not really here." Lennon chuckled. "I may not be here in the flesh, but I'm here in spirit. And I'm happy to help you out, man. You remind me a lot of myself when I was your age." Over the next few weeks, Ben found himself spending more and more time with the ghost of John Lennon. They would talk for hours about music, politics, and life in general, and Ben was amazed at how insightful and wise Lennon was, even in death. Despite the fact that Lennon was no longer alive, Ben found that their conversations were some of the most inspiring and meaningful of his life. Lennon would offer him advice and encouragement, and he would push him to take risks and be true to himself as an artist. As their friendship deepened, Ben began to realize that he was developing a deep admiration and affection for Lennon, not just as a musician, but as a person. He felt a sense of kinship with him, like they were kindred spirits, and he found himself wishing that he could have met him in person, back when he was still alive. But as much as he admired and respected Lennon, Ben knew that he couldn't let their relationship distract him from his music. He continued to work on his compositions, pushing himself to new heights of creativity and expression, and he knew that Lennon would have been proud of him. In the end, Ben's relationship with the ghost of John Lennon became one of the defining experiences of his life. Even though he knew that Lennon was no longer alive, he felt like he had a mentor and a friend who was always there to support him, no matter what. And as his music continued to reach new heights of popularity and success, he knew that he owed a great deal of his success to the inspiration and guidance of John Lennon's ghost. But Ben knew the truth, and he didn't let the naysayers get to him. In fact, he found that his friendship with Lennon's ghost only made him more determined to prove himself as an artist. He poured all of his energy and passion into his music, pushing himself harder than ever before, and he knew that Lennon would have approved. As the years passed, Ben's musical career continued to soar, and he became one of the most respected and influential musicians of his generation. He won awards and accolades for his compositions, and he became a role model and inspiration to countless young musicians around the world. Through it all, he never forgot the lessons he had learned from John Lennon's ghost. He remembered the importance of staying true to oneself, of standing up for what was right, and of using one's art to make a difference in the world. And even though Lennon was no longer with him in the flesh, he knew that his spirit would always be there to guide him, to inspire him, and to remind him of the power of music to change the world.
